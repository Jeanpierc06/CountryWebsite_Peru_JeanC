History of Peru

The Inca Empire emerged in the early 13th century and reached its peak between 1438 and 1533. It extended across western South America, encompassing modern-day Peru, Ecuador, Bolivia, Chile, and parts of Argentina and Colombia. The empire was known for its sophisticated administrative systems, monumental architecture, and extensive road networks despit having no contact to any nations outside the continent. Cusco was the heart and capital of the empire, where the ruler lived and the administrative, political, and military center of the empire and the incas built a sophisticated network of roads and forts/stone architecture like the fortress of Sacsayhuaman and the main plaza, Huacaypata.


Spanish Conquest.


The Spanish conquest of the Inca Empire, led by Francisco Pizarro starting in 1532, was a swift, takeover facilitated by European diseases (like smallpox), which lowered the natives population a ton due to no immunity or kind of interference with those diseases before unlike the europeans. The spanish also had superior weaponry (steel, guns, horses), and the Inca Empire's internal weakness from a recent civil war which the spanish empire took advantage of. 68 Spanish soldiers under conquistador Francisco Pizarro, along with his brothers in arms and their indigenous allies, captured the last Sapa Inca, Atahualpa, at the Battle of Cajamarca in 1532. It was the first step in a long campaign that took decades of fighting but ended in Spanish victory in 1572 and colonization of the region as the Viceroyalty of Peru.

Independence of the republic of Peru

Spain ruled over Peru and most of South America for 300 years from the 1530's until 1800's Where Peru's independence was declared on July 28, 1821, by José de San Martín in Lima along with another Spanish colonies, marking the end of Spanish colonial rule and the fight for Independence. The Peruvian War of Independence spanned from 1809 to 1826, involving various military conflicts and uprisings against Spanish forces. San Martín's declaration emphasized the will of the people for freedom, and he created the first Peruvian flag. The struggle for independence was influenced by earlier rebellions, notably led by Túpac Amaru II, which laid the groundwork for the eventual liberation
